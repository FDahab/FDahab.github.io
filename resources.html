Here are some seminal and interesting papers that I recommend, they will generally be HEP papers, because I don't feel comfortable recommending papers in areas that are not my own. Each paper includes a brief description of its importance and relevance.

"Infrared Photons and Gravitons" - Steven Weinberg

Many important ideas in Field Theory were introduced or are explained by this paper. The idea of amplitudes not being observables quite exactly, and the existence physically redundant diagrams. As well as the idea of resumming perturbative contributions and how an order-by-order divergent amplitude might actually be associated with a vanishing resummed ampltiude.

"A planar diagram theory for strong interactions" - Gerardus 't Hooft

While QCD is very neat in the sense that the Lagrangian is very simple, understanding its features from a quantitative point of view is generally a very difficult task, especially when the matter is its spectrum and other situations where trusty perturbation theory breaks down. This paper introduced the idea of the large N limit of SU(N) gauge theories, and how tremendously some of these calculations are simplified when we take it.

"Baryons in the 1/N expansion" - Edward Witten

In very Witten-esque fashion, Witten describes the ideas of 't Hooft's paper in incredible clarity, and also understand how to extend some of the ideas initially applied to mesons only, to try to understand how baryons behave in this regime.

"Renormalization and Effective Lagrangians" - Joseph Polchinski

The usual Wilsonian picture of the Renormalization Group and how to find the Renormalization Group Equations is very simple and intuitive, but most of the times, it's impossible to actually apply. Polchinski introduces the idea of smooth cutoffs and how we can use them to find the Exact Renormalization Group Equations

"Gedanken Worlds without Higgs: QCD-Induced Electroweak Symmetry Breaking" - Chris Quigg, Robert Shrock

A lot to learn about the Standard Model on this one. How would our world change were the Higgs not present. Particularly, this is a great journal club paper.

Quantum Field Theory

Graduate Level

"Quantum Field Theory and the Standard Model", Matthew D. Schwartz

In my opinion probably the best overall introductory book to QFT. I particularly like the renormalization section, though the renormalization group discussion is quite brief. But it's not without its flaws, the introductory chapters are rather flimsy, and I suggest looking for complementary sources when reading them.

"A Modern Introduction to Quantum Field Theory", Michele Maggiore

A shorter book, but also pretty good, in particular the author is somewhat careful about group representations in Field Theory, and is, in my opinion, a good primer on them. It's no substitute for Weinberg in this sense, who is a lot more thorough in this aspect, but it's a simpler first look with a lot better notation. The author also

"Quantum Field Theory", Lewis H. Ryder

An older book, but it has it's gems, in particular, the construction of the Faddeev-Popov procedure is the most thorough I've seen in any, even modern, textbook.

Here are some recommended online resources for various topics in physics. Each recommendation includes a brief description of its usefulness and relevance.

AlphaXiV

Courtesy of a few students from Stanford, AlphaXiV is an open forum that allows people to engage in discussions directly under papers on the ArXiV. It's still very new, but it has potential to be a great tool, allowing for better feedback after pre-prints are published, as well as creating more direct pathways for people to discuss new ideas. It's hard to say whether it will live up to its potential, but I'm praying that it does.

Clear Physics

This is a website by a user on PhysicsStackExchange who's answers I generally find nice. Present in this website are a variety of nice discussions on a variety of topics from introductory classical mechanics, all the way to quite complicated topics in Quantum Field Theory. Reading other people's perspective on Physics is always nice, so do read their discussion on topics that interest you.

David Tong: Lectures on Theoretical Physics

The ever expanding compendium of excellent lecture notes and problem sets by Tong. You can find most usual topics in Physics there, from first-year undergraduate level, all the way to pretty advanced graduate topics. URL: href="https://www.damtp.cam.ac.uk/user/tong/teaching.html"> David Tong: Lectures on Theoretical Physics

Physics StackExchange

Physics is very hard, and questions are often overlapping when we're learning fairly common topics, PSE offers a platform where people studying physics can help each other. There are already a lot of nice answers to a variety of questions, but you can always ask more, use it to its fullest. (There are also some not so nice ones but that's bound to happen)
