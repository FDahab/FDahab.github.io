Beyond Standard Model Physics

The Standard Model is an excellent Effective Field Theory, able to match, to an obscene level of precision, any high energy physics experiment we are currently able to make. Nevertheless, it is undeniably incomplete, there are simply too many things outside colliders it is not able to predict. To name a few: there are no mechanisms to give mass to neutrinos in the Standard Model; no UV complete theory of Quantum Gravity; no explanation to fine tuning/hierarchy problems; and many more.

Evidently then, there's much work to be done, both theoretical and experimental. How do we build models to address a few of these shortcomings? Given models that do, where will their deviations from Standard Model predictions manifest most strongly given what we can do experimentally? How do we build better experiments or better utilize the ones we already have? These are all equally important questions, and we need as a community, people to work on all of them, and most of all, work together. I am currently more interested in the theoretical side of things, working on trying to answer the first two questions, particularly given that the next generation of colliders will be precision machines.

Quantum Field Theory and its non-perturbative aspects

Quantum Field Theory is (arguably, but not really arguable) the most powerful framework we currently have in physics, able to unite the many triumphs of both Quantum Mechanics and Special Relativity. However, at this point, its applications have transcended that scope, with people working on ideas ranging from condensed matter physics to even pure math.

QFT is clearly incredibly versatile with many people are working on many different aspects of it. Yet, even though we've come a long way, there is a lot we don't understand about it. We are particularly ignorant when we cannot use pertubative expansions, be it because we have no small parameter to expand around, or because the behaviour is genuinely non-analytic. There are clear big gaps in our understanding. Well, what about it, these questions sound simply like math abstractions, we are supposed to be physicists! Well, ways to address and understand bits and pieces of these non-perturbative regimes can give us very insightful clues about nature, from the non-perturbative regime of QCD and the origins of confinement, to the understanding of the asymmetry between matter and antimatter in the uninverse. In a very concrete sense, understanding better our toolset is important if we are to apply this toolset to explain nature (or model it, depending on your philosophical stance).

Scattering Amplitudes

Although extremely predictive, Quantum Field Theory forces us to pay a price for building local and manifest Lorentz Invariant Lagrangians. Particularly, if our theory includes massless particles with spin greater or equal to 1, Gauge Invariance is imposed onto us so the degrees of freedom match. This, especially for non-Abelian Gauge Theories, makes building amplitudes extremely cumbersome, we need to not only consider the complicated Gauge Boson - Gauge Boson interactions, but as Faddeev and Popov taught us, we need unphysical ghost as well.

For a 4 gluon amplitude in Yang Mills, if we are to compute the cross-section in the context of QFT, we would need to deal with well over 1000 terms, and yet, upon simplification, the result is incredibly simple. A natural question is then, surely there's a better way of doing this? The program of On-Shell Amplitudes is precisely dedicated to ideas like this. By imposing only the most fundamental restrictions to the behaviour of the amplitude, how much of its form can one deduce without having to jump through the hoops of QFT. And can we learn new lessons about nature by doing so? This to me is profoundly interesting.
